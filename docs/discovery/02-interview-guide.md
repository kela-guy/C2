# 02 — Problem-Discovery Interview Guide

Deliverable for to-do `interview`. A JTBD-style discussion guide for problem validation. Goal: understand how commanders set up a new base today, where it hurts, and how long it takes — WITHOUT pitching the auto-coverage solution.

## Rules of engagement (read before every session)

- Ask about PAST behavior, not future hypotheticals ("walk me through the last time…", never "would you use…").
- Listen 80%, talk 20%. Follow pain with "why?" up to 5 times.
- Do NOT describe Kela's scan/score/drag-drop idea. If asked, say "we're studying the problem first."
- Capture verbatim quotes and the time/effort numbers they volunteer.
- No leading questions ("isn't manual scanning slow?" → instead "how long did that take?").

## Session structure (50 min)

### 0. Warm-up (5 min)
- Role, base type, how long responsible. How many first-time setups have you led?

### 1. Replay the last real setup (15 min) — generative core
- "Walk me through the last time you took over / stood up a site from scratch. Start from day one."
- Probe each step:
  - How did you figure out the threats and likely approach directions?
  - How did you find dead zones / blind spots / weak points? What tools (paper maps, recon, GIS, gut)?
  - How did you decide where each asset went? In what order?
  - Who else was involved and what did they hand you?
- Time/effort: "From arrival to 'I felt the site was covered,' how long? Hours, days, weeks?"

### 2. Pain & workarounds (10 min)
- "What was the most frustrating part of that process?"
- "Where did you feel unsure whether the layout was actually good?"
- "What did you do to double-check coverage? Did you ever get it wrong?"
- "What workarounds or homemade tools do you use (spreadsheets, range tables, overlays)?"

### 3. Judging 'covered' (10 min) — feeds A2/A3
- "How do you personally decide a site is 'protected enough'? What does 'enough' mean?"
- "Do you think about air and ground threats the same way or separately?"
- "Have you ever felt a site looked covered on paper but wasn't? What happened?"

### 4. Terrain & constraints (7 min) — feeds A2 credibility
- "How much does terrain (hills, valleys, structures) change your placement?"
- "What real-world constraints limit where you can actually put an asset (power, access, line of fire, mutual support)?"

### 5. Wrap (3 min)
- "If a magic assistant could do one part of this for you, which part?" (only open-ended desire, no solution shown)
- Referral ask (snowball).

## Question bank (swap in as needed)

- "Tell me more about that…" / "Why did that matter?"
- "What happened right before / right after that step?"
- "How often does that go wrong?"
- "Who taught you to do it that way?"

## Anti-patterns to avoid (from discovery skill)

- Confirmation bias: also recruit and probe skeptics; ask "when would an auto-plan be a bad idea?"
- Fake validation: never ask "would you use X?" — measure real past behavior.
- Pitching: zero solution talk until synthesis is done.

## Synthesis template (fill after each session, then roll up)

Per participant:
- Time-to-covered (their number): ____
- Tools used today: ____
- Top 3 pains (verbatim): ____
- How they judge "covered": ____
- Terrain/constraint factors: ____
- Surprises / disconfirming evidence: ____

Roll-up (after ≥5 per segment):
- Pattern table: pain → frequency → segments affected.
- Evidence for/against the core problem (cite counts, e.g. "5/6 Primary used paper/GIS by hand").
- Opportunities discovered → map to Opportunity Solution Tree in the discovery plan (section 7).

## Exit criteria for this to-do

- [ ] ≥5 interviews completed per segment, synthesized
- [ ] Pain patterns quantified (counts per segment), with verbatim quotes
- [ ] Baseline "time-to-covered today" captured (used for A4 success metric)
- [ ] Updated evidence for/against the problem written into the discovery findings
